Design notes: the 8 questions

Each card restates a requirement from the design brief, then shows exactly where it is manifest in this prototype.

Q1Should it even be AI?

Requirement

Help DfE analysts and statisticians — highly educated civil servants who write for professional and academic audiences — publish CMS content that a lay reader can access and understand. They are stuck (no accessibility or plain-language expertise) and burdened (time poor). AI addresses this by creating a first pass of simplified, well-structured alternatives and acting to remove the manual checking work. AI is not strictly required (a scoring system could exist), but users would want to try it — with ways to check its work. Success: automated accessibility checks find no errors, reading age falls from 16 to 12, comprehension rises in real-world testing.

Where it shows in this prototype

  • Creates: every note offers a drafted rewrite of the author's own words (notes 1, 3, 4, 6) — never new content.
  • Acts: checking runs continuously in the background; no manual "run checker" step is needed in the default mode.
  • Success measures are on screen: the toolbar chips track Reading age (starts at "about 16", falls towards 12 as suggestions are accepted) and Accessibility checks (2 issues → no errors).
  • Ways to check it: every suggestion carries a confidence level, a reason and a source, and nothing applies without a preview.

Q2Which mental model fits? — the book editor

Requirement

A book editor: takes my words and improves them, never changes meaning, asks for my opinion, and accepts my decisions. A partner with particular skills whose job is to help me improve my work.

Where it shows in this prototype

  • The whole aesthetic is a manuscript on an editor's desk: a cream paper page set in a book serif, a plain grey desk around it, and the editor working in blue pencil — wavy underlines, numbered marks, notes "In the margin", and galley-proof previews (struck-through old text, pencilled insertions with a caret ⌃).
  • Takes my words and improves them: rewrites reuse the author's own facts and figures; note 5 refuses to invent alt text.
  • Never changes meaning: each preview keeps the original visible (struck through) beside the proposal so meaning can be compared; note 1 says so explicitly.
  • Asks for my opinion: note 2 asks a genuine editorial question instead of guessing.
  • Accepts my decisions: "Keep mine" is on every note; the editor records the decision and stops flagging that phrase in the document.
  • The manuscript stays yours: the page is directly editable in place (title and body, with a formatting toolbar for bold, italic, headings, lists and links). If you rewrite a marked sentence yourself, EESy tracks your latest wording, so Undo and "Keep mine" always restore exactly what you wrote — the editor annotates the manuscript, never the other way round.

Q3How salient is AI?

Requirement

In between: visible and giving advice, but the user's work is the hero — the AI only advises. Visible value statement: "Do you want to check if your content can be accessed and understood by everyone?" It highlights issues subtly and gives options and reasons where there are opportunities to improve.

Where it shows in this prototype

  • The work is the hero: the manuscript takes the widest column, in the largest, darkest type; EESy lives in a narrower margin in a quieter voice and never overlays the text.
  • Visible but subtle: marks are thin wavy underlines with small numbered buttons — no red banners, no pop-ups over the writing.
  • The value statement appears word-for-word at the top of the margin and in the first-run note.
  • Options and reasons: every note explains why it was made and offers choices rather than a single verdict.

Q4What is the form factor?

Requirement

Ambient / proactive.

Where it shows in this prototype

  • There is no chat window and no wand button to summon the AI: EESy is always present in the margin, reading alongside the author.
  • Select "Continue writing" in the editor to see the proactive behaviour: a new paragraph appears and, a moment later, EESy quietly marks it (note 6) without being asked.
  • The ambience is adjustable, not inescapable — see Q5.

Q5How much guidance?

Requirement — three essentials

  • This tool helps you make content accessible to all and easy to understand for a layperson.
  • It won't change anything without your permission.
  • It won't create anything new for you — it relies on your expertise and knowledge.

Three assumptions to correct

  • It can't check for accuracy.
  • It can't write things for you.
  • It can't guarantee anything — only help you understand how to help.

Where in the journey

It can interject when the user makes a mistake, but this can be turned off or down; it can scan after writing or highlight as they go; it should adjust as the user learns.

Where it shows in this prototype

  • First run: the opening "note from your editor" states the three essentials and the three corrections, before any marks appear.
  • Reinforced in context: the manuscript is stamped "EESy never changes it without your permission"; note 5 declines to write alt text; the capability line under the notes repeats what EESy cannot do.
  • Turn it off or down: the toolbar radio group — As I write / After I finish / Off — moves marking from live interjection, to scan-after-writing, to silent.
  • Adjusts as they learn: "Keep mine" teaches EESy to stop flagging that phrase; the memory panel shows preferences carried between sessions.

Q6How human should it be?

Requirement

Not a persona — EESy is a product name, not a character. Tone: neutral but conversational, a hint of warmth, direct; helpful but not encouraging. Competency: intermediate. Users cannot adjust how human it feels. Humanising element: memory of past encounters, to help the user recall how they solved problems. Known risk: feeling too human annoys people with bad past experiences and implies more help than exists.

Where it shows in this prototype

  • No avatar, no face, no small talk — just marks and notes, like a real editor's pencil. There is no setting to make it chattier, by design.
  • Tone throughout: "Why I marked this…", "I will not guess", "Kept as written." Direct, plain, no praise and no exclamation marks.
  • Intermediate competency made visible: confidence tags (high / medium), and hedges such as "This is a judgement call, so I am less certain than usual."
  • Memory: the "From your past sessions" panel recalls how the author solved the same problem in May, and the standing preferences they set.
  • Managing the risk: the capability line caps expectations ("it can be wrong — you decide what changes").

Q7How much control?

Requirement

All five tactics: Explain, Preview, Clarify, Consent, Undo/Override. Position on the automation spectrum: AI proposes, user decides — the user reviews and approves before anything happens. Rationale: the audience are experts whose names sit on the release; an editor who changed a statistician's meaning uninvited would be fired, so every change must pass through the author's hands.

Where it shows in this prototype

  • Explain: every note opens with "Why I marked this".
  • Preview: "Preview change" shows the galley proof in place — struck-through original, pencilled insertion — before Accept unlocks. Accept is disabled until you have previewed.
  • Clarify: note 2 asks which definition of "disadvantaged" you mean and waits.
  • Consent: the marks radio group is standing consent for future behaviour; nothing is ever auto-applied.
  • Undo/Override: every resolved note gains an Undo button that restores your original wording exactly; "Keep mine" overrides the advice.

Q8How to manage trust?

Requirement

All four tactics: capability reminders, confidence levels, source transparency, reasoning explanations.

Where it shows in this prototype

  • Capability reminders: in the first-run note and pinned under the margin — "EESy… cannot check that your figures are accurate… it can be wrong."
  • Confidence levels: a High or Medium tag on every note, with lower confidence on judgement calls (structure) than on rules (link text).
  • Source transparency: each note cites its basis — the GOV.UK content style guide, the department's plain English standard, or the specific WCAG 2.2 success criterion.
  • Reasoning explanations: the "Why I marked this" line on every note.
