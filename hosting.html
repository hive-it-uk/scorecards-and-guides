How we score

Scores use the Online Ecosystem Ethics Framework v2.1 (Hive IT, 2025–26). Thirteen criteria span three pillars: Environmental (criteria 1–4 and 13), Social and supply chain (criteria 5–8), and Ethics and governance (criteria 9–12).

Scoring scale

Each criterion is scored 0–4: 0 = no information or no effort; 1 = minimal; 2 = developing; 3 = established; 4 = leading practice.

Assurance levels

Every score carries an assurance level, shown next to it as a flag on the evidence — never multiplied into the score: Verified — checked by an independent third party (audit, certification, court or regulatory record, peer-reviewed study, or established watchdog); Self-reported — the provider's own disclosure, not independently checked; Estimated — our reasoned judgement from public information; Unknown — no reliable evidence found. A criterion that applies but is not disclosed scores 0 with Unknown and is flagged; one that does not apply is excluded from the total — a gap is never penalised twice.

How scores combine

Scores combine in two stages. Each pillar is the average of its applicable criteria. The three pillar scores are then combined using a weighted geometric mean, so a weak pillar drags the overall result down and cannot be fully hidden by strong ones. A pillar scoring 0 is floored at 0.01 — a fixed, framework-wide constant — and the result is flagged as having a weak pillar, so the score degrades sharply but the cause stays visible. Equal pillar weights are the default. If you adjust the weights, the overall figure is shown under your chosen weights and, for comparison, under equal weights: if the two differ a lot, the result depends heavily on the weighting, and the pillar profile is the more honest guide.

Source priority

Where independent sources (court filings, regulatory enforcement actions, watchdog reports, investigative journalism, peer-reviewed studies) conflict with a provider's own disclosures, the independent source takes precedence and can lower a self-reported score. Corporate sustainability reports are a starting point, not a conclusion.

Criterion 1 — carbon (dual-track accounting)

Location-based grid averages are used for annual compliance reporting. For operational decisions — workload routing, data-centre siting — Locational Marginal Emissions (LME) provide a more precise measure: the carbon cost of the next unit of electricity at a specific grid node, accounting for which generators are actually dispatched. Providers who demonstrate verified LME-aware workload scheduling are scored higher under both criterion 1 and criterion 3. All carbon figures are estimates with wide honest uncertainty — published estimates for the same digital activity can differ by orders of magnitude — so state the model and version used and treat single figures as indicative.

Criterion 2 — water (dual-scope footprinting)

Assessed under ISO 14046 principles, distinguishing direct water (on-site evaporative cooling, measured by WUE) from indirect water (off-site electricity generation and hardware manufacturing — often the larger share). Local water stress is assessed using AWARE weighting: consumption in a water-stressed region carries greater impact than the same volume in an abundant one.

Criterion 9 — transparency (verification threshold)

Transparency is both its own criterion and the foundation for every other score. A raw score of 3 or 4 requires independent verification — a third-party audit, regulatory finding, independent benchmark, established watchdog documentation, or fully open artefacts that independent parties can inspect directly. Detailed self-reporting without any independent checking is capped at 2, however thorough it appears. Verification does not require money: fully open artefacts (an open licence with weights, training data or a complete data statement, and code all public), an independent benchmark such as the Stanford FMTI, or a peer-reviewed evaluation all count — the cap rewards openness, not audit budgets.

Criterion 13 — per-unit infrastructure efficiency

For hosting providers this measures how efficiently a unit of compute is delivered. 4 = independently verified PUE and energy-mix figures with full methodology; 3 = detailed self-reported PUE, WUE and energy mix; 2 = partial figures; 1 = limited disclosure; 0 = no data or known high-carbon infrastructure. PUE (Power Usage Effectiveness) and WUE (Water Usage Effectiveness) are the primary metrics. Key anchors: Hetzner air-cooled = WUE near zero; Scaleway PAR-DC3 WUE 0.00009 L/kWh; DigitalOcean: no data published.

Military restraint

A high score on criterion 10 means stronger published limits on harmful military and weapons use. A low score means fewer restrictions, more permissive use, or complicity in autonomous weapons or civilian targeting — not more contracts as such.

Red flags

Serious concerns — for example complicity in human-rights abuses, enabling autonomous weapons systems, or falsifying impact data — are recorded as red flags and reported separately, on the card, never blended into the numerical score. A single number can never hide a serious problem. A red flag requires at least one of: a UN body finding, a regulatory sanction, a court ruling, credible established-watchdog reporting, or an independent human-rights impact assessment.

No provider in this scorecard currently meets this threshold. The absence of a red flag does not mean no concerns exist — see the individual scores.

Location adjustments

The scorecard can adjust two criteria for where you are deploying: carbon (criterion 1) reflects the carbon intensity of the local electricity grid, and privacy (criterion 12) reflects data-residency and legal-jurisdiction risk. UK and EU deployments apply stricter CLOUD Act penalties for US-jurisdiction providers.

Coverage and review

Scores were last reviewed in July 2026, following a full evidence audit. Assessments carry a 12-month validity window. Evidence coverage is shown as the percentage of applicable criteria where the assurance level is not Unknown. Missing information is a finding — it means a provider is not publishing what a responsible procurement decision requires.

Limitations

  • Many scores are Estimated because of limited disclosure. A criterion that applies but is undisclosed scores 0 — which itself signals a problem.
  • Training and inference carbon are separated where the data permits.
  • Criteria 4 (repairability) and 5 (conflict minerals) sit at the data-centre hardware layer. Providers who own their hardware have more direct control than those using colocation. Server auction programmes (for example Hetzner's) are meaningful direct action.

Standards referenced: GHG Protocol; Software Carbon Intensity (ISO/IEC 21031:2024); ISO 14046 (water); ISO/IEC 30134 series (PUE/WUE); EU Regulation 2017/821 (conflict minerals); EU AI Act Article 5; WCAG 2.2; GDPR (EU Regulation 2016/679).

Acronyms and abbreviations

B Corp
Certified B Corporation. An independent certification from B Lab that verifies a company meets standards of social and environmental performance, accountability, and transparency.
BSI
British Standards Institution. A UK-based independent body that carries out ISO audits and certifications.
Carbon neutral
Net zero carbon emissions for a defined scope, achieved by a combination of reduction and verified offsets.
Climate positive / Carbon negative
Removes more carbon from the atmosphere than it emits — a stronger claim than carbon neutral.
CFE
Carbon-free Energy. Energy from sources that produce no direct carbon emissions, including renewables (wind, solar, hydro) and nuclear.
CLOUD Act
Clarifying Lawful Overseas Use of Data Act (US, 2018). Allows US authorities to demand data from US-owned companies regardless of where that data is physically stored. Applies to AWS (Amazon), Google Cloud, and Microsoft Azure.
Colocation (colo)
A data centre where a provider rents space, power, and connectivity rather than owning the building. The host controls the servers; the facility operator controls the building.
CSRD
Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (EU). Requires large companies to disclose environmental and social impact under standardised rules. Came into force from 2024.
ERF
Energy Recovery Factor. The proportion of a data centre's total energy input that is recovered and reused — for example, as heat for nearby homes or swimming pools. An ERF of 0.95 means 95% of energy is reused.
GCP
Google Cloud Platform. The cloud infrastructure and hosting services sold by Google.
GDPR / UK GDPR
General Data Protection Regulation. EU law (GDPR) and UK equivalent (UK GDPR) governing how personal data is collected, stored, and used. The EU-UK adequacy decision currently allows transfers between the two.
GWF
Green Web Foundation. An independent non-profit that verifies hosting providers' renewable energy claims and publishes results publicly at thegreenwebfoundation.org.
ISO 14001
International standard for environmental management systems. Requires organisations to identify and control their environmental impact.
ISO 14068-1
International standard for carbon neutrality claims and methodology (published 2023). Stricter than earlier frameworks; requires third-party verification.
ISO 27001
International standard for information security management. Covers how organisations protect data.
LCA
Life Cycle Assessment. A methodology for measuring the environmental impact of a product or service across its full life — from manufacture through use to disposal. Governed by ISO 14040/44.
LEED
Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design. A US-originated green building certification for energy-efficient, low-impact facilities.
PUE
Power Usage Effectiveness. The ratio of total data centre power draw to the power actually used by IT equipment. A PUE of 1.0 is perfect (no overhead). Industry average is around 1.56; best-in-class data centres achieve 1.06–1.10.
REGO
Renewable Energy Guarantee of Origin. A UK certificate that proves a unit of electricity came from a renewable source. Quality varies — some REGOs are traded separately from the actual electricity.
RMAP
Responsible Minerals Assurance Process. An industry audit programme for the ethical sourcing of minerals — particularly 3TG (tin, tantalum, tungsten, gold) from conflict-affected regions.
SBTi
Science Based Targets initiative. An independent body that validates corporate emissions reduction targets to ensure they align with climate science (the Paris Agreement).
Scope 1 emissions
Direct greenhouse gas emissions from sources owned or controlled by the organisation — for example, on-site generators or company vehicles.
Scope 2 emissions
Indirect emissions from purchased energy — electricity, heat, or cooling bought from external suppliers.
Scope 3 emissions
All other indirect emissions in the value chain — including hardware manufacture, supply chain, employee travel, and the emissions caused by customers using the service.
WUE
Water Usage Effectiveness. Litres of water consumed per kilowatt-hour of IT equipment power. Lower is better. A WUE near zero indicates air or adiabatic cooling with no evaporative water loss.

The framework: 13 criteria

Version 2.1. Thirteen criteria in three pillars. Each is scored 0–4; every score carries an assurance level. Released under CC0 — no rights reserved.

A. Environmental
1Carbon emissions●●●○

The greenhouse gases released because the thing exists and runs — measured as carbon dioxide equivalent (CO₂e).

Why it matters. Computing is a fast-growing source of emissions. Training and running AI models, and powering data centres, use large and rising amounts of electricity.

A standing note on uncertainty. Every carbon figure is an estimate. Digital carbon models allocate shares of past, measured emissions — they do not predict how totals would change if you acted differently — and published estimates for the same activity can differ by orders of magnitude. When the Sustainable Web Design model moved from version 3 to version 4, its energy-per-byte figure fell by roughly two-thirds. State the model and version used; treat single figures as indicative.

What to look at. Three-scope reporting (Scope 1 direct, Scope 2 energy, Scope 3 supply chain and use); embodied carbon in hardware; carbon intensity of electricity, hour-by-hour rather than annual matching; real reductions versus offsets; science-based targets (SBTi); per-use Software Carbon Intensity (SCI) score.

LME note. Annual average grid figures are adequate for compliance but can misrepresent real impact. Locational Marginal Emissions (LMEs) measure the carbon cost of the next unit of electricity at a specific grid point and time. Providers who use LME signals for operational workload scheduling (criterion 3) are doing something substantively more rigorous than purchasing annual renewable energy certificates. See: Cote and Sun (2025), arXiv:2512.18819 — a preprint, not yet peer reviewed. LME factors are valid only for small load changes and are noisy, so LME-awareness is treated as a practice signal, not an accounting method — vendor LME-derived carbon numbers stay self-reported.

AI note. Separate the training footprint (one-off, large) from the inference footprint (ongoing, per query). Ask for both, and ask how training cost is spread across expected use.

Scoring signals. 4 = full three-scope reporting including embodied carbon, both training and inference disclosed, science-based targets, hour-by-hour clean energy, independently verified. 0–1 = no data, or claims resting mainly on offsets.

2Water use●●○○

The fresh water consumed to run and cool the infrastructure — assessed across both direct on-site use and indirect off-site use, in line with ISO 14046.

Why it matters. Data centres use water to cool equipment. Roughly two-thirds of a data centre's total water footprint occurs off-site at the power stations that generate its electricity. Water stress is local: the same litre matters far more in a drought-hit region than in a wet one. Generative AI generates significant heat: estimates suggest roughly 0.5 litres of freshwater per 20–50 typical queries.

Two scopes of water use (ISO 14046). Direct (on-site, Scope 1): water evaporated in cooling towers and humidification systems, measured by WUE. Indirect (off-site, Scope 2 and 3): water at electricity generation (thermoelectric and hydro plants) and in hardware manufacturing (semiconductor fabrication is highly water-intensive). These off-site volumes often exceed on-site consumption.

What to look at. Both water withdrawal (total taken) and water consumption (permanently lost); per-site figures, not just a company total; AWARE water-stress weighting at stressed sites; indirect use alongside direct; recycled or non-drinking water and closed-loop cooling; verified WUE.

Scoring signals. 4 = per-site reporting of both direct and indirect use, ISO 14046 aligned, AWARE-weighted at water-stressed sites, verified replenishment. 0–1 = no data, only a single global on-site figure, or no attention to local stress.

3Energy & resource efficiency●●○○

How much useful work the thing does for the energy and hardware it uses.

Why it matters. Efficiency reduces every other environmental impact at once — less energy means less carbon, less water and less hardware. It is often the single most practical lever.

What to look at. Data centres: PUE (Power Usage Effectiveness) — lower is better. Hardware: server utilisation, not idle servers. Software: efficient code, sensible defaults, caching, minimal loading. AI: energy per query, smaller or distilled models where adequate, carbon-aware running. Reuse of waste heat.

Standards. ISO/IEC 30134-2 (PUE) and the wider ISO/IEC 30134 series; Software Carbon Intensity (SCI, ISO/IEC 21031:2024); EU data-centre reporting scheme (Delegated Regulation (EU) 2024/1364).

Scoring signals. 4 = measured and published efficiency metrics, strong results, waste-heat reuse, verified carbon-aware operation. 0–1 = no efficiency data, obvious waste.

4Repairability & e-waste●○○○

How long the underlying hardware lasts, whether it can be repaired and upgraded, and what happens to it at end of life.

Why it matters. Making hardware carries a large environmental cost, so using it longer spreads that cost over more useful work. E-waste is one of the fastest-growing waste streams in the world.

What to look at. How long equipment is kept before replacement; repair, refurbishment and reuse programmes; spare parts and repair information; responsible recycling and take-back; refurbished or second-life hardware. Software-driven obsolescence: whether software drops support for older hardware faster than necessary, pushing usable devices into the bin.

Standards. EU Ecodesign Regulation (EU) 2024/1781; Right to Repair Directive (EU) 2024/1799; WEEE Directive 2012/19/EU; Global E-waste Monitor.

Scoring signals. 4 = long hardware life, repair and reuse programmes, published e-waste recycling rates, long software support periods. 0–1 = short replacement cycles, no repair support, software that drops support for usable hardware.

13Per-unit efficiency●○○○

The carbon and water cost of a single query — the ongoing environmental impact of tool use, as opposed to aggregate corporate reporting.

Why it matters. Criteria 1 and 2 measure corporate commitments; criterion 3 measures infrastructure efficiency. Neither tells you what it costs in carbon and water every time a user sends a message. At scale — a team using an AI tool hundreds of times a day — per-prompt figures become the primary operational variable. They also reveal something important about transparency.

What to look at. Published per-query energy, carbon and water figures with a clear methodology; coverage of both direct energy/carbon and operational water; independent or peer review; a defined reproducible prompt type; disclosure for reasoning or extended-thinking modes (which carry significantly higher costs); distinction between market-based and location-based carbon accounting.

Workload optimisation. Some providers shift workloads in response to real-time grid conditions: temporal shifting (scheduling batch work to off-peak, cleaner hours) and spatial shifting (routing non-latency-sensitive queries to lower-LME regions). Verified LME-aware or carbon-aware scheduling is a significant positive indicator here and under criterion 3. Vague claims of "carbon-aware compute" without operational evidence should be treated as self-reported at best.

Reference figures. Google: ~0.24 Wh, ~0.26 mL, ~0.03 g CO₂ per median text prompt (most detailed public methodology). Mistral Large 2: 1.14 g CO₂e and 45 mL per 400-token response (peer-reviewed lifecycle assessment, ADEME/Carbone 4, ISO 14040/44 — the most independently verified per-inference figures as of 2026). OpenAI: ~0.34 Wh and ~0.32 mL per average ChatGPT query (self-reported, 2024, no peer review or carbon figure).

Standards. Green Software Foundation Software Carbon Intensity (SCI, ISO/IEC 21031:2024).

Scoring signals. 4 = peer-reviewed figures for both carbon and water per prompt with a clear reproducible methodology. 3 = independently verified or detailed self-reported figures for both. 2 = partial figures or credible third-party estimates. 1 = no per-query data; impact assumed typical. 0 = no data and known use of high-carbon energy for inference.

B. Social and supply chain
5Conflict minerals●○○○

Whether the materials in the hardware — especially tin, tantalum, tungsten and gold (3TG), plus cobalt — are sourced without fuelling conflict or abuse.

Why it matters. Some minerals come from areas where mining funds armed groups or relies on dangerous and exploitative labour, including child labour.

What to look at. OECD five-step due-diligence method; named audited smelters and conflict-free sources; cobalt coverage; public reporting and action when problems are found; Responsible Minerals Assurance Process.

Standards. OECD Due Diligence Guidance; EU Conflict Minerals Regulation (EU) 2017/821; Responsible Minerals Initiative; UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights; EU Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (EU) 2024/1760; US Dodd-Frank Act §1502.

Scoring signals. 4 = full OECD-aligned due diligence, named audited smelters, cobalt covered, independent assurance and public reporting. 0–1 = no due diligence and no disclosure.

6Local communities●○○○

The effect on the people who live near the physical infrastructure — mainly data centres.

Why it matters. Data centres compete with local people for water, electricity, land and grid capacity. They can bring jobs and investment, but can also strain scarce water and be built without real consultation.

What to look at. Genuine consultation before building; honest assessment of effects on local water, electricity prices and grid capacity; shared benefits (jobs, investment, waste heat); land use and housing; human-rights impact assessment; a working complaints route.

Standards. UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights; IFC Performance Standards; OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises.

Scoring signals. 4 = early real consultation, honest local impact assessment, shared local benefit and a working complaints route. 0–1 = building with little consultation, no local impact assessment, or clear local harm.

7Workers & labour●○○○

The treatment of people who do the work behind the ecosystem — in mines and factories, in data centres, and in digital work such as data labelling and content moderation.

Why it matters. The online world depends on much hidden human labour. Data-labelling and content-moderation workers are often low-paid, with little protection, sometimes facing distressing material.

What to look at. Pay, hours, safety and security across the supply chain; conditions for data-labelling and moderation workers (including mental-health support); freedom to join a union; action against forced and child labour backed by audits; standards applied to contractors. Algorithmic management: whether automated systems that track, score or discipline workers respect their dignity and allow meaningful appeal.

Standards. ILO Declaration on Fundamental Principles and Rights at Work; UN Guiding Principles; EU Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (EU) 2024/1760.

Scoring signals. 4 = fair pay and conditions throughout the chain, support for moderation workers, humane and appealable automated management, respected union rights and audited freedom from forced and child labour. 0–1 = no standards beyond direct staff, intrusive automated management with no appeal.

8Accessibility●●○○

Whether the thing can be used by people with disabilities, and whether it widens or narrows access generally.

Why it matters. A large share of people have a disability. Inaccessible tools shut people out — including from public services. Accessibility is also a legal duty for many organisations.

What to look at. WCAG 2.2 AA conformance; an honest accessibility statement; testing with assistive technology and disabled users, not just automated checks. For AI: fair performance across languages and regions — whether a model works as well, and is as safe, in low-resource languages as in English.

Standards. WCAG 2.2 (W3C / ISO/IEC 40500:2025); EN 301 549 (European Accessibility Act basis); GOV.UK Service Manual accessibility guidance.

Scoring signals. 4 = tested WCAG 2.2 AA conformance, honest statement, testing with disabled users, and (for AI) measured quality and safety across languages. 0–1 = no accessibility work or AI that works well only for a narrow group.

C. Ethics and governance
9Transparency●●○○

How open the provider is about how the thing works and what it costs the world — and whether anyone can hold it to account.

Why it matters. Every other criterion depends on this one. If a provider tells you nothing, you cannot judge its water use, emissions, labour practices or military links.

What to look at. Regular public reporting on environmental, social and governance impacts; AI model cards or system cards; for software: open-source licensing and open governance; independent audits and certifications; honesty about problems and limits; a clear accountable owner.

Verification threshold. A score of 3 or 4 requires independent verification — an audit by a credible third party, a regulatory finding, documentation from an established watchdog, an independent benchmark (for example the Stanford Foundation Model Transparency Index), or fully open artefacts (licence, weights, training data or a complete data statement, and code, all public) that independent parties can inspect directly. Detailed self-reporting without independent checking scores no higher than 2, however thorough. This is the only criterion in the framework with a hard score cap tied to evidence type — and the open-artefact route costs nothing, so the cap rewards openness, not audit budgets.

Standards. NIST AI Risk Management Framework; EU AI Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689); ISO/IEC 42001; OECD AI Principles; GRI; EU Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (EU) 2022/2464; Open Source Initiative.

Scoring signals. 4 = full reporting independently audited, clear AI documentation, open governance, proactive disclosure of problems. 3 = substantive reporting with some independent verification. 2 = detailed self-reporting, no independent verification — the ceiling without third-party checking. 1 = limited or patchy disclosure. 0 = no meaningful disclosure.

10Military use●○○○

Whether the thing is used by militaries or in weapons, and how strong the provider's rules are for limiting harmful military use.

Why it matters. Online infrastructure and AI are increasingly used in defence and warfare. Systems that select and attack targets without meaningful human control raise grave concerns under international law.

What to look at. Known military or defence contracts and use in weapons or targeting systems. Limits: a clear, enforced acceptable-use policy ruling out weapons that operate without meaningful human control or use to harm civilians. Export-control compliance. Openness about defence relationships.

Red flag default. A product triggers an un-bypassable red flag if it directly enables the selection of, or attack on, human targets without meaningful human control, or is knowingly used to target civilians — whatever the vendor's policy says.

Standards. UN CCW Group of Governmental Experts on lethal autonomous weapons; ICRC position on autonomous weapon systems; EU Dual-Use Regulation (EU) 2021/821; Wassenaar Arrangement.

Scoring signals. 4 = clear enforced limits, no loopholes, meaningful human control, full export-control compliance, openness. 0–1 = no limits, no transparency, or use in systems that harm civilians or lack human control.

11Political involvement●○○○

How the provider tries to shape law, policy and politics through lobbying, donations and government relationships.

Why it matters. Large technology firms spend heavily to influence the rules that govern them. Hidden influence is the concern, not engagement itself.

What to look at. Lobbying activity on official registers; political donations and funding of trade groups and think tanks; whether public positions match private lobbying; government contracts and the revolving door between firm and regulators; responsibility for effects on elections and public debate.

Standards. EU Transparency Register; UK Office of the Registrar of Consultant Lobbyists; US Lobbying Disclosure Act filings and OpenSecrets; Canada Registry of Lobbyists; OECD lobbying principles internationally. Use the register for the jurisdiction whose influence you are assessing.

Scoring signals. 4 = full disclosure of lobbying, donations and government ties; public positions match private ones; responsibility for platform effects on democracy. 0–1 = hidden influence, or public claims contradicting private lobbying.

12Privacy & data governance●●●○

How the thing collects, uses, protects and shares people's data.

Why it matters. Poor privacy and weak security expose people to surveillance, profiling, breaches and harm. AI adds questions about training data and personal information models may hold or reveal.

What to look at. Lawful basis and data minimisation; clear privacy information and real user control (access, correction, deletion, portability); strong security and prompt breach reporting; data location and government access. For AI — score these separately: (1) User-query privacy: is input used to train future models without consent? How long is it stored? (2) Training-data provenance: was personal data or copyrighted work taken without consent, licence or compensation? A model can guard user queries perfectly while being built on data scraped without permission.

Standards. GDPR (Regulation (EU) 2016/679); UK Data Protection Act 2018; ISO/IEC 27001; EU AI Act.

Scoring signals. 4 = strong lawful data practices, real user control, certified security, honest breach handling, strong on both user-query privacy and training-data provenance. 0–1 = unclear data collection, weak security, no training data transparency.
